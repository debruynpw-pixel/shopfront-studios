How Much Does a Website Cost in South Africa? (2026 Prices)

By PW de Bruyn 21 July 2026 7 min read

Ask five people what a website costs in South Africa and you will get five answers between R500 and R80,000. That is not because anyone is lying. It is because "a website" describes a one-page landing page for a mobile car wash and a 200-product online store with payment gateway integration, and those two things share about as much DNA as a bakkie and a bus. Here is what each option actually costs in 2026, and what makes the number move.

Short answer

A small business website in South Africa costs R2,500 to R8,000 from a freelancer, R8,000 to R25,000 from a small agency, and R40,000 or more from a large agency. DIY builders like Wix or Squarespace run R150 to R400 per month. Add roughly R150 to R250 a year for a .co.za domain.

What does a small business website actually cost in South Africa?

There are four honest price bands in the local market. Each one exists because it serves a different kind of business at a different stage.

DIY builders: R150 to R400 per month. Wix, Squarespace, Shopify and their kin. You pick a template, you type in your own words, you upload your own photos. The subscription covers hosting, SSL and the editor. There is no once-off build fee because you are the builder. Over three years you will pay somewhere between R5,400 and R14,400, and you will have spent a lot of your own evenings.

Freelancers: R2,500 to R8,000 once-off. This is the most common route for a South African trade or small service business. You get a real person, usually working on a template or a light custom layout, delivering three to eight pages. At the bottom of that range expect to supply your own copy and photos. At the top you might get basic copy assistance and a contact form that actually lands in your inbox.

Small agencies and studios: R8,000 to R25,000. Here you are paying for a process rather than a person. Discovery, a design pass, copy written for you, proper mobile testing, on-page SEO set up before launch, and someone who answers the phone in month four. Studios that build static rather than on WordPress tend to sit lower in this band and carry no maintenance retainer, since there are no plugins to keep patched.

Larger agencies: R40,000 and up. Custom design from scratch, brand work, content strategy, integrations with your CRM or booking system, sometimes a custom CMS. Legitimate money for legitimate scope, but overkill for a plumber in Boksburg.

Ecommerce sits on top of all of it. Add roughly R6,000 to R20,000 over the equivalent brochure site for a working store: product setup, a payment gateway like PayFast, Yoco or Peach, shipping rules, and order emails.

Why do web design quotes vary so much?

Five things drive almost all of the variance. When a quote surprises you, one of these is usually the reason.

Page count. A one-page site and a fifteen-page site are not the same job. Every extra page is layout, copy, images and testing. Most quotes assume five to seven pages unless stated.

Custom design versus template. A template costs the developer a few hours of adaptation. A custom design costs days of layout work before a single line of code exists. This single choice can double a quote.

Copywriting. Writing the actual words is often 30% of the real work and it is the thing clients most often assume is included. It usually is not, below about R8,000.

Integrations. Booking calendars, payment gateways, CRM connections, WhatsApp click-to-chat, live stock feeds. Each one is a small project with its own testing.

Ecommerce. Products, variants, tax, shipping zones, and the fact that a broken checkout costs real money means it gets tested far harder than a contact form.

Notice that the DIY column is not the loser. If you are testing a business idea, have no budget, and need something live this week, Wix is the correct call and anyone telling you otherwise is selling. The tradeoff is time and a monthly bill that never ends. The moment your site needs to rank, load fast on a rural 3G connection, or do something a template does not do, you have outgrown it.

What are the hidden costs nobody quotes you for?

The build fee is the headline. These are the line items that show up later.

  • Domain: R150 – R250 a year for a .co.za, R200 – R350 for a .com. Annual, forever. Register it in your own name.
  • Hosting: R60 – R250 a month for shared local hosting. Static sites can sit on free tiers. Builders bundle it into the subscription.
  • SSL certificate: usually R0. Let's Encrypt is free and most decent hosts include it. If someone quotes you R1,200 a year for SSL, ask why.
  • Business email: R60 – R120 per user per month for Google Workspace or Microsoft 365. Worth every cent versus a gmail address on your bakkie.
  • Copywriting: R800 – R2,500 per page if bought separately. This is the most commonly underestimated cost.
  • Photography: R2,000 – R6,000 for a half-day shoot. Stock photos of American plumbers do not build trust with a Joburg homeowner.
  • Maintenance: R400 – R1,500 a month on WordPress, where plugins and core updates genuinely need attention. Static sites need close to nothing.
  • Changes after launch. Ask what a text edit costs before you sign. R350 per small change is normal; R350 to fix a typo they introduced is not.

How do you know if a quote is fair?

You cannot judge a quote by its number alone. You judge it by what the number buys. Four questions sort the good quotes from the bad ones fast.

Does it list page count and revision rounds? A quote that says "website: R12,000" and nothing else is not a quote, it is a hope. Real quotes name the pages and cap the revisions, usually at two rounds.

Who owns the domain and the files? The answer must be you. If a developer registers your domain on their account, they hold your business hostage without meaning to. Ask for it in writing.

What happens in month six? Get the post-launch cost in the quote. Hosting, changes, response time. Silence here is how a R6,000 site quietly becomes a R14,000 site.

Is there any mention of getting found? A site nobody visits is expensive at any price. The quote should mention page titles, meta descriptions, a sitemap, mobile speed and a Google Business Profile link at minimum. If it does not, you are buying a brochure, not a lead source.

One more thing worth saying plainly. The cheapest quote and the most expensive quote are both fine outcomes. What is not fine is a quote you cannot explain to someone else. If you cannot say what you are buying and why it costs that, ask again before you pay a deposit.

Frequently asked questions

Is a R2,500 website worth it?

It can be, if the scope matches the price. At R2,500 you should expect a single-page or three-page site on a template, your own copy, and stock or supplied photos. That is genuinely useful for a plumber or a hairdresser who needs a credible link to send people. What you should not expect at that price is custom design, written copy, or ongoing support.

How much does a .co.za domain cost per year in South Africa?

A .co.za domain costs roughly R150 to R250 per year from most South African registrars, and a .com usually lands between R200 and R350 per year. Register it in your own name and with your own email address, not your developer's. Domain ownership is the one thing you should never hand over.

Do I have to pay a monthly fee for a website?

Not always. Builders like Wix and Squarespace are subscription-only, so R150 to R400 per month is unavoidable. A custom-built static site can run on hosting of R60 to R150 per month, and some hosts are free for simple sites. The monthly fee you cannot skip is your domain renewal, billed annually.

How long does it take to build a small business website in South Africa?

A DIY builder site takes a weekend to a fortnight of your own evenings. A freelancer typically delivers in one to three weeks. A small agency usually runs two to four weeks for a standard business site, and larger agency projects with custom design and integrations commonly take six to twelve weeks.
