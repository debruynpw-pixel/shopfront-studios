Short answer

A salon website needs online booking that works on a phone, a gallery of your own work sorted by service, profiles for each stylist with their speciality, a service menu, your Instagram feed, your cancellation and deposit policy, and clear directions with parking. Booking is the point. Everything else supports it.

How people actually choose a salon

Almost nobody walks past your window and decides to come in anymore. They see a set of blonde balayage photos on Instagram at half past nine at night, they tap through to your profile, and then they start checking whether you are a real business they can trust with their hair.

The two questions in their head are simple. Can I see work on hair like mine? And can I book without phoning someone? If they have 4C hair and every photo on your feed is a straight blow wave, they leave. If they have to DM you and wait until tomorrow morning for a reply, half of them book somewhere else before you have even read the message.

This is why walk-in trade keeps shrinking and booking links keep winning. Someone in Melville or Sea Point decides at 22:40 that they want a cut on Saturday. The salon that gets that booking is the one whose link opened a calendar instead of a chat window. Nothing about the quality of your work decided it. The link did.

Referrals behave the same way. When a client tells her colleague about you, the colleague does not phone. She searches your name, finds your page, looks for the stylist her friend mentioned, and books that person. If your site cannot name your stylists, that referral goes to whoever is free, and sometimes it goes nowhere.

Why most salon websites lose the booking

Instagram is the whole business and booking happens in the DMs. Enquiries pile up overnight, some get buried under story replies, and the ones you answer at 8am have already booked elsewhere.

A booking page that takes the slot the moment they decide. Instagram stays your shopfront, the site becomes your till.

No gallery of actual work, or a gallery of stock photos of women who have never sat in your chair. New clients cannot tell whether you have done their hair type before.

A real gallery of your work grouped by service, so someone looking for knotless braids or a fade sees exactly that, not a shuffle of everything you have ever posted.

No online booking, so every slot depends on someone answering a phone during trading hours. Evenings, Sundays and public holidays are dead time when your clients are actually free to plan.

A 24-hour calendar tied to your real availability, with deposits taken up front if you want them, so a booked slot is a committed slot.

No stylist profiles. A client loved what Thandi did last time but cannot request her, so she gets whoever is available and the second visit never feels like the first.

A page per stylist with their speciality and their own work, bookable by name. Loyalty attaches to a person, and that person keeps their column full.

What we build into a salon site

Online booking, wired in properly

Fresha, Booksy, Acuity or whatever you already run, embedded so a client picks stylist, service and time on their phone in under a minute. Deposit rules and buffer times honoured. This is the single most important thing on the site and everything else points at it.

Gallery sorted by service

Not one big grid. Separate sets for colour, braids, locs, fades, nails, lashes and treatments, so someone can find their exact hair type and see three or four results before they commit. Before-and-after pairs where you have them.

Stylist profiles

Photo, name, what they are known for, how long they have been doing it, and a book-with-them button. Clients request people, not shops. This also protects you when a stylist is on leave because the client sees who else does the same work.

A service menu that suits you

Every treatment listed with a plain description and how long to set aside. Whether you publish your own prices, show a range, or mark colour and extensions as consultation-based is entirely your decision and we build the menu to match.

Live Instagram feed

Your latest posts pulled onto the site automatically, so the page never looks abandoned even in a quiet month. New visitors see this week's work, not a gallery you last updated in 2023.

Policy, location and parking

Cancellation window, no-show and deposit terms written plainly so you can point at them instead of arguing. Plus a map, the actual entrance, whether there is secure parking, and which centre or street you are in. Awkward parking loses first-time clients.

How a salon gets found in South Africa

Three things bring new clients to a salon website, and they are not equally weighted. Instagram brings the discovery, Google brings the intent, and word of mouth brings the best clients you will ever have.

Google is where the ready-to-book searches happen. "Hair salon near me", "barber Woodstock", "braiding salon Sandton", "nail bar Durban North". These searches almost always show the map pack first, which means your Google Business Profile does more work than your homepage. Get the category right, load real photos of the actual space and the actual work, keep your hours accurate over December, and ask every happy client for a review while they are still in the chair. Our guide on setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa walks through the whole thing, including the review habit that most salons never build.

Your website's job in that chain is to close. Someone taps your listing, lands on your page, sees work on hair like theirs, finds the stylist their friend mentioned, and books. A site that only says "call us for appointments" wastes every bit of that traffic.

The suburb matters too. If you serve Fourways, name Fourways. Mention the centre, the nearby areas you draw from, and the landmarks people actually use. A salon page that just says "we are based in Johannesburg" competes with the whole city instead of owning its patch.

Frequently asked questions

Do I still need a website if my salon is busy on Instagram?

Yes, because Instagram is where people find you and a website is where they book you. A DM enquiry sits unread until someone opens the app. A booking page takes the slot at 22:40 on a Sunday while you are asleep. Keep posting on Instagram, then send every profile-link tap to a page that closes the booking.

Can you connect my website to Fresha or Booksy?

Yes. We embed Fresha, Booksy, Acuity or your existing system so clients pick a stylist, a service and a time without leaving your site. If you have no booking system yet, we will help you choose one that suits your team size and your deposit rules, then wire it in properly on mobile.

Do I have to publish my service prices on the website?

That is your call and we build it either way. A full price list filters out people shopping on price alone. A service menu with no figures gets more enquiries but more time-wasters. Many salons list prices for standard cuts and treatments and mark colour, braids and extensions as consultation-based since length changes everything.
