Websites for Auto Repair Shops and Panel Beaters

The driver with the knocking engine or the dented bakkie is already on their phone. Your site should answer their three questions — do you work on my car, do you deal with my insurer, will you quote me first — before they scroll past you.

Short answer

An auto repair shop website needs the makes you service listed by name, the insurers you are approved with, your RMI or MIWA accreditation, real photos of finished jobs, your hours and service areas, and a tap-to-call number plus a quote request visible without scrolling. Everything else is decoration.

How people actually pick a workshop

Nobody browses for a mechanic on a Sunday afternoon for fun. There is a noise, a warning light, a leak on the driveway, or a bumper hanging off after someone reversed into them in the Centurion Mall parking. They pull out a phone, search "mechanic near me" or "panel beater Boksburg", and start tapping listings.

Watch what they do next. They are not reading your company story. They are scanning for their own car's badge. A Hilux owner is looking for the word Toyota. A woman with a 2018 Polo wants to see VW written somewhere before she phones. If it is not there in the first few seconds, she backs out and taps the next result — not because you cannot do the job, but because she has no way of knowing you can.

Panel beating runs on a second question, and it is bigger. After a bump the driver is not really shopping for a workshop, they are shopping for someone who will handle the claim without making it their problem. "Are you approved by my insurer?" decides the job. If your site names Santam, OUTsurance, Discovery Insure, MiWay, Old Mutual Insure — whoever you are actually approved with — you have already won half the conversation. If it says nothing, they phone their broker and go where the broker sends them.

Third question: will they be told what it costs before you start? Every South African driver has a story about a workshop that stripped the car and then phoned with a number. A clear line saying you inspect, quote in writing, and only start once the owner approves removes more hesitation than any amount of polish. Same with a courtesy car — if you have one, or a lift to the Gautrain station, say so. That is often the deciding detail for someone who still has to get to work tomorrow.

And they are doing all of this one-handed, standing next to a car that will not start. Tap-to-call must be at the top. Not in a footer, not behind a contact page.

Why most workshop websites lose the enquiry

No list of makes serviced, so every visitor assumes you do not do theirs.

A plain makes-serviced block naming every brand you touch — Toyota, VW, Ford, Isuzu, Hyundai, Kia, Mercedes, BMW, whatever is on your ramps — with a line on where you are the specialist. Costs nothing, stops the guesswork.

Nothing about insurers, so claim work goes to the shop down the road that says it.

Your approved insurers named on the page, your RMI or MIWA accreditation shown as a badge, and a short three-step explanation of how a claim runs through your workshop from assessment to handover.

Stock photos of a spotless American workshop with a guy in a red cap.

Your ramps, your team, your signage, your suburb. We build the site around photos you take on a phone. A slightly rough real photo of your bay converts better than a perfect stock one, every time.

No before-and-after photos, so your best argument is invisible.

A job gallery with before and after pairs — the crumpled quarter panel next to the resprayed one. This is the single most persuasive thing a panel beater owns and most sites do not have it.

Phone number buried in the footer under a contact form nobody fills in.

Tap-to-call and WhatsApp fixed at the top of every page and floating on mobile, so a stressed driver reaches you in one tap instead of scrolling for your number.

One page for a workshop that pulls cars from six suburbs.

Service-area pages for the suburbs you actually draw from — Boksburg, Benoni, Roodepoort, Centurion — each with its own directions, landmarks and hours, so you show up when someone searches with a suburb name attached.

How a workshop gets found in South Africa

Most of your new work comes from one of two places: someone searching a suburb plus a service, or someone tapping the map pack at the top of Google. The map pack is not your website — it is your Google Business Profile, and it is the highest-value thing you can fix this week. Correct category (auto repair shop, or auto body shop for panel beating), correct hours including Saturdays, photos of the actual premises, and a steady trickle of reviews. Our guide to setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa walks through it step by step, and it costs nothing but an afternoon.

Your website's job is to back that up. Google cross-checks the name, address and phone number on your site against your profile, so they must match exactly — same street, same suite number, same format. A workshop with three branches needs three pages with three distinct addresses, not one page listing all of them in a paragraph.

Then the search terms themselves. Drivers do not search "automotive repair solutions". They search "panel beater near me", "cambelt Roodepoort", "clutch replacement Benoni", "insurance approved panel beater Boksburg", "roadworthy Centurion". Each of those is a page or a section with those words on it in plain English. Write out your services the way a customer says them, not the way a parts catalogue does.

Reviews do heavy lifting in this trade because trust is the whole purchase. Ask every customer at handover, while the car is clean and they are relieved. And speed matters: half your visitors are on mobile data on a roadside, so a site that takes eight seconds to load has already lost them to the workshop next door.

Frequently asked questions

What does an auto repair shop website actually need?

A list of the makes you service, the insurers you are approved with, your RMI or MIWA accreditation, photos of jobs you have finished, your workshop hours, and a tap-to-call number and quote form at the top of the page. Most people are on a phone with a broken car, so everything must be reachable in one thumb movement.

Do I need to list every make I work on?

Yes. If a Ford Ranger owner cannot see the word Ford on your site, they assume you do not touch Fords and they phone the next workshop. We build a plain makes-serviced block naming every brand you handle, and a short note where you are a specialist, so nobody has to guess.

Can the site show that we are insurance approved?

It should be one of the first things a visitor sees. We put your approved insurers and your RMI or MIWA accreditation high on the page as named badges, plus a short explanation of how a claim runs through your shop. For panel beaters this is the single strongest trust signal you have.
