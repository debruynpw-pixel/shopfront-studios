Websites for Electricians and Electrical Contractors

Two very different people land on your site: one has no power right now, the other needs a COC before a transfer goes through. Both are in a hurry. This is a site built so neither of them has to look for your number.

Short answer

An electrician's website needs a tappable phone number and WhatsApp button on the first screen, a clear Certificate of Compliance section, your wireman's licence and ECA or ECB registration shown openly, the suburbs you work in listed by name, photos of your own DB boards and installations, and a plain statement of when you answer after-hours calls.

How people actually look for an electrician

Electrical enquiries split into two streams, and they behave nothing alike. Confusing them is why so many electrician sites feel busy and still ring quietly.

The first stream is the emergency. Half the house is dead, the DB board trips the second it is pushed up, there is a burning smell behind a plug point, or the geyser isolator has cooked itself. This person is standing in a passage with a torch on their phone. They search "electrician near me", tap the first two or three listings, and phone whoever answers. They will not read your About page. If your number is an image, or sits in a footer, or opens a form, they are already dialling somebody else.

The second stream is compliance, and it is urgent in a quieter way. Someone is selling a house and the attorney has told them the transfer cannot proceed without a valid Certificate of Compliance. There is a date on that transfer. They search "COC certificate" plus their suburb, and what they are really checking is whether you are legitimately allowed to issue one and how quickly you can get there. This person will read. They want the wireman's licence number, the registration, and some evidence that you have done this before. Give them that and the call is a formality.

Then there is the third thing load shedding built: backup power. Inverter and solar installation has become one of the biggest search categories in the trade, and it does not behave like either of the others. Nobody buys an inverter at 11pm in a panic. They research for weeks, get three quotes, ask friends, and look hard at whether the installer is a real registered electrician or a bakkie with a sticker. That means photos of finished installs, batteries mounted properly, cable work you are not embarrassed by, and an explanation of what a system does and does not run.

One site has to serve all three without making any of them work for it.

Why most electrician websites lose the call

No mention of COC anywhere on the site, or one word on a services list.

Certificate of Compliance gets its own section and its own page, written for someone whose transfer is waiting. What the inspection covers, what happens when faults are found, how soon you can be there, and what they get at the end. It is one of the highest-intent searches in the trade and most of your competitors have left it on the table.

No registration or licence number, so the customer cannot verify you without phoning to ask.

Wireman's licence number, ECA or ECB membership, and a photograph of the certificate, placed where a nervous seller or estate agent will see it. Verification anxiety is the single biggest silent drop-off on a compliance enquiry.

"We service Gauteng." Nobody searches for Gauteng.

Suburbs named individually. Randburg, Roodepoort, Northcliff, Centurion, Boksburg, Kempton Park, Krugersdorp. Named suburbs match how people actually search, and they tell the customer you are close enough to arrive today, which is the real question behind the question.

A contact form as the only way to reach you, when the customer has no lights.

Tap-to-call in the header on every page, a fixed WhatsApp button that follows the scroll, and a plain line about after-hours availability. WhatsApp also lets them photograph the tripped board and the burnt plug, which tells you more in one image than five minutes of description.

What we build into an electrical contractor site

Tap-to-call and WhatsApp above the fold

Both live in the header and stay there while the page scrolls. On a phone the number is a link, not text and not an image, so one tap dials. This is the whole ball game for fault calls.

A proper COC section

Certificate of Compliance treated as a service people search for by name, with its own page, its own enquiry route, and an explanation aimed at sellers, buyers, agents and landlords who each need one for different reasons.

Registration and licence on display

Wireman's licence number, ECA or ECB membership, and public liability cover, shown in the header area and repeated near every enquiry point rather than hidden on an About page nobody opens.

Inverter and solar backup section

A dedicated area for backup power: what a system realistically runs, how the changeover works, finished installs in local homes, and a separate enquiry route because these leads need follow-up, not a callout.

Suburbs served, listed by name

Every suburb you actually work in, written out. It matches real searches, sets response expectations, and gives Google the local signals a generic province page never will.

Real job photos, fast loading

Your boards, your trenching, your inverter installs, compressed properly so the page opens on mobile data in a Roodepoort driveway. Stock photos of a smiling man with a clipboard convince nobody.

How an electrician gets found in South Africa

Most of your work will not arrive through the website directly. It arrives through the Google map pack, the three listings that appear when somebody types "electrician Randburg" or "COC certificate near me". Your Google Business Profile is what puts you in that pack, and the website is what the customer checks once they have tapped through. The two work as a pair: the profile wins the impression, the site wins the call. We cover how to set the profile up properly, which categories to pick and how reviews actually move you in the Google Business Profile guide for South African businesses.

The categories matter more than people expect. Electrician, electrical installation service and solar energy contractor are separate categories, and picking the right primary one changes which searches you appear for. If half your income is now backup power, that should be reflected in your profile and in a page on your site that Google can match to it.

Reviews are the other lever, and electricians are in an unusually good position to collect them. A COC job ends with a satisfied person whose transfer just cleared. That is the moment to ask, not a week later. Reviews that mention the suburb and the job type read as credible to humans and give Google matching text to work with.

Then there is the part people forget: speed. A site that takes eight seconds to load loses the emergency caller entirely, because they have already gone back and tapped the next result. Fast, light pages are not a technical nicety in this trade, they are the difference between the phone ringing and not.

Frequently asked questions

What does an electrician's website need in South Africa?

A tappable phone number and WhatsApp button on the first screen, a clear Certificate of Compliance section, your ECA or ECB membership and wireman's licence number, the suburbs you actually work in listed by name, photos of your own boards and installations, and a stated position on after-hours callouts. Everything else is secondary.

Should a COC get its own page?

Yes. Certificate of Compliance is one of the highest-intent searches in the trade, usually typed by someone whose property transfer is waiting on it. That person is not browsing, they are booking. A dedicated COC page that explains what the inspection covers, what happens if faults are found, and how fast you can get out, converts far better than a line buried on a services page.

Do I need to show my registration and licence numbers?

Show them. Customers and estate agents have been burned by people issuing COCs they were never entitled to issue, so they check. Printing your wireman's licence number and your ECA or ECB membership on the site, next to a photo of the certificate, removes the doubt before anyone has to phone and ask an awkward question.

Is a solar and inverter page worth building?

For most Gauteng and Cape electricians it is now the busiest search category on the site. Backup power enquiries behave differently to faults: people research for weeks, compare installers, and want to see finished installs and a registered electrician behind the work. It needs its own page with real photos, not a bullet on the homepage.
