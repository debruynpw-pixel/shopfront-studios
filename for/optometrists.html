How people actually choose an optometrist

Nobody has an eye emergency at 2am and searches for an optometrist. That is the whole problem. An annual eye test is a low-urgency errand competing with work, school runs and everything else, and it has been postponed once already. The patient is not urgently motivated. They are mildly motivated, on their phone, in a gap between other things — and any friction sends the task back onto the "later" pile where it dies.

So the sequence is short and unforgiving. They search something like "optometrist near me" or "eye test Centurion", they tap two or three results, and they scan for one thing first: do you take my medical aid? Optometry benefits sit in a specific pocket of most schemes, and patients have learned the hard way that going to a non-contracted practice means paying cash and claiming back, or not claiming at all. If your site does not answer that question, they leave. They do not phone to ask. They open the next tab.

If you clear the medical aid hurdle, the next questions come quickly. Can I book without phoning? What actually happens in the test — is anything going to be uncomfortable? Do you stock frames I would actually wear, or is it three rows of the same thing? And if they already wear contact lenses, can they reorder their usual boxes without repeating the whole conversation each time.

Worth noting: a decent share of the people scanning your site are not the patient. They are a daughter booking for a parent whose vision has changed, or a parent booking a school-age child after a teacher flagged squinting at the board. Those people need slightly different things — access and parking, whether you test children, how long to set aside.

Why most optometry websites lose the booking

No medical aid list anywhere on the site, or a vague "we accept most medical aids".

Name the schemes. A plain list — Discovery, Bonitas, Momentum, Bestmed, GEMS, Medihelp, whichever you are contracted with — on its own page and repeated on the booking form. "Most" is not an answer to a patient who only wants to know about theirs, and it costs you the ones who assume the answer is no.

Booking is a phone number, and the phone is only answered 08:30 to 16:30 on weekdays.

Those are exactly the hours your patients are also at work. We put a booking request form or a live calendar on the site so the appointment gets captured at 21:00 on a Tuesday, when they actually remembered. Your front desk confirms it in the morning.

Nothing explains what an eye test involves, so the patient imagines the worst.

A short, calm page: how long it takes, what the machines do, that the puff-of-air test is brief, whether drops are used and what that means for driving afterwards. People postpone what they cannot picture. Describing it plainly removes a reason to delay.

Stock photos of models in glasses that have nothing to do with your practice.

Photographs of your actual rooms, your actual dispensary, your actual frame wall and the people behind the counter. Patients are deciding whether a place feels competent and unhurried. A stock image tells them nothing; a real one tells them what to expect when they walk in.

What we build into an optometry practice site

Medical aid schemes, named

A clear list of the schemes you are contracted with, kept easy for your staff to update when a contract changes, and shown again at the point of booking so nobody arrives with the wrong assumption.

Online appointment booking

A booking flow that works properly on a phone: service type, preferred day, medical aid, contact details. Either a request form your front desk confirms, or a live calendar synced to your practice diary.

The eye test, explained

A plain-language walkthrough of a comprehensive eye examination — duration, the equipment used, whether dilating drops are involved, what to bring, and what happens if a script changes. Written to reduce hesitation, not to sell.

Frames and lens brands stocked

The frame houses and lens brands you actually carry, with sunglasses and prescription ranges separated. Patients want to know whether it is worth the drive before they commit an afternoon.

Contact lens repeat supply

A repeat-order form that captures the existing prescription details, lens type and preferred collection or delivery, so regular wearers reorder in a minute instead of phoning during their lunch break.

Practitioner and practice details

The practising optometrist's name, qualification and HPCSA registration number, practice number, hours including Saturday trading, plus directions, parking and wheelchair access — the details that decide whether an older patient books at all.

How an optometry practice gets found in South Africa

Most of your new patients arrive through one of two doors: a local search on Google, or a Google Business Profile listing that shows up on the map before they ever reach a website. For a suburb practice — Fourways, Claremont, Menlyn, Umhlanga — the map pack usually matters more than the website ranking does, because that is where the phone number, the hours and the reviews live. Getting that listing complete, correctly categorised and consistently addressed is the highest-return hour you will spend; our guide to setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa covers the steps, including the verification part that trips most practices up.

The website's job is what happens after the tap. It should load fast on a mid-range Android on mobile data, state the suburb and surrounding areas in ordinary sentences rather than a stuffed list, and have a page for each thing people search separately — eye tests, children's vision, contact lenses, sunglasses, and the medical aid question on its own page, because "optometrist that takes Discovery" is a real search that deserves a real answer.

One honest constraint. The HPCSA's ethical rules restrict how registered practitioners may advertise and canvass for patients, and that shapes what a practice site can say. We treat these websites as informational and access-focused: factual descriptions of services, real details about the practice, and an easy way to book. Not superlatives, not comparisons to other practitioners, not clinical outcome claims, not patient testimonials about results. That is a limit worth respecting on its own terms — and in practice it costs you nothing, because the patient scanning your site wants facts and a booking button anyway. Check your final wording against the current HPCSA guidelines or with your professional body; we will build to whatever they tell you.

Frequently asked questions

What should an optometrist's website include in South Africa?

It should list the medical aid schemes the practice is contracted with, allow online appointment booking, explain what an eye test involves and how long it takes, show the frame and lens brands stocked, state practice hours, and display the practising optometrist's name and HPCSA registration number. Parking and wheelchair access details matter too.

Can an optometry practice advertise on its website under HPCSA rules?

The HPCSA's ethical rules limit how registered practitioners may advertise or canvass for patients. In practice, a practice website is expected to be factual and informational rather than promotional, so it should avoid superlatives, comparative claims about other practitioners, testimonials about clinical outcomes, and anything that could be read as touting. We build these sites to inform and give access. Confirm your own wording with the HPCSA guidelines or your professional body.

Should we list which medical aids we accept on the website?

Yes. Medical aid acceptance is the first thing most patients check before booking an eye test. If Discovery, Bonitas, Momentum, Bestmed or GEMS members cannot tell within a few seconds whether you are contracted with their scheme, many will simply phone the next practice on the list instead of asking.

Do patients actually use online booking for eye tests?

An annual eye test is easy to postpone, so anything that adds friction loses the appointment. Many patients think about booking after hours or during their own working day, when phoning is awkward. A booking form or live calendar captures those people instead of asking them to remember to call tomorrow.
