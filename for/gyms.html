Short answer

The timetable is the most visited page.

How people actually choose a gym

The January spike is real, and so is the smaller one in late September when people realise December is coming. Those weeks are when someone who has not trained in two years opens three tabs and compares three gyms in your area, all in about eleven minutes, usually on a phone, usually at night.

They are checking three things. First, the contract. Can I get out of this, or am I locked in for twelve months with a debit order I will resent by March? Second, the timetable. Is there a class at 06:00 before work, or at 18:15 after I have fought the N1, and does it happen on the days I can actually go? Third, and nobody says this out loud, will I feel like an idiot in there? A first-timer is genuinely worried about walking into a room full of people who know what they are doing.

Your timetable page will get more views than your homepage. Not once, either. Existing members come back to it every week, and prospects check it three or four times before they commit. If it is wrong, or if it is a PDF that downloads instead of opening, you have annoyed the people who already pay you and lost the ones who were about to.

The other thing to understand is that almost nobody signs up cold. They want to come in once first. A free week, a day pass, a first class free. That first visit is where the sale actually happens, and the website's only real job is to get them booked in for it.

Why most gym websites lose the member

A PDF timetable from three months ago. Classes have moved, two instructors have left, and a prospect who plans their week around the wrong 06:00 slot pitches up to a locked door.

A live timetable you edit yourself in a minute from your phone. Filter by class type, day and trainer, and it opens in the browser instead of downloading a file nobody wants.

The only call to action is "contact us" or "enquire about membership". That asks a nervous first-timer to commit to a sales conversation before they have even seen the place.

A free trial or day pass as the main button on every screen. One short form, an instant confirmation, and their name in your follow-up list. Low commitment in, real conversion out.

Contract terms are nowhere on the site. People assume the worst, remember every horror story about cancelling a gym contract in South Africa, and go with the competitor who was upfront.

Terms written plainly: length, notice period, joining fee, freeze policy, month-to-month options. Being straight about it is a competitive advantage here, not a risk.

Stock photos of a chrome American gym with models who have never been to Kempton Park. Members arrive, see the real place, and feel misled before their first session.

Real photos of your floor, your rig, your studio, your parking and your actual members. Honest beats glossy. People want to know exactly what they are walking into.

What we build into a gym site

Live class timetable

The page that matters most. Filterable by day, class type and trainer, readable on a phone without pinching, and editable by your front desk in under a minute. No developer, no PDF, no three-month-old schedule.

Trial and day-pass capture

Your primary conversion, not an afterthought. Name, number, which class or day they want, done in three fields. They get a confirmation, you get a warm lead with a reason to phone them. This replaces "contact us" everywhere on the site.

Trainer bios and qualifications

Photo, real credentials, what they specialise in, and who they are good with. A REPSSA registration or a rehab speciality matters to the person coming back from an injury. For personal trainers this page is the whole business.

Real facility photos

Your equipment, your studio floor, your change rooms, your parking. Shot in your space, not bought from a stock library. This is what settles the "will I feel out of place here" question before they arrive.

Membership options explained

Each option laid out with what it actually includes, who it suits and what the commitment is. Whether you publish your own figures or route everyone to an enquiry is your decision, and the layout works either way.

Class booking, location and parking

Members reserve a spot so your 18:15 HIIT does not overflow, connected to whatever system you run. Plus a map, the right entrance, secure parking, and the suburbs you draw from. Parking decides more gym memberships than anyone admits.

How a gym gets found in South Africa

Gym search is almost entirely local and almost entirely map-driven. "Gym near me", "pilates studio Claremont", "boxing gym Pretoria East", "personal trainer Umhlanga". Google shows the map pack above everything else, so your Google Business Profile is doing more heavy lifting than any page on your site.

Get it right and it compounds. Correct category, real photos of the actual floor, hours that stay accurate over the December shutdown, and a steady flow of reviews from members who are already happy. Our guide on setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa covers the setup and the review habit, which is the part most gyms skip and then wonder why the gym down the road outranks them.

Then time your run. The January searches start in the last week of December, and there is a second wave when the schools go back. Your timetable needs to be current and your trial offer needs to be live before that, not scrambled together in the second week of January when the traffic has already been and gone.

Name your area properly too. If you draw from Edenvale, Bedfordview and Greenstone, say those words on the page. A gym is a distance-and-traffic decision, and people search the way they think about their commute.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most important page on a gym website?

The class timetable, by a long way. People are checking whether your 06:00 or your 18:15 fits around work and traffic before they consider anything else. It must be live, correct, readable on a phone, and editable by your own staff in under a minute. A PDF timetable is the fastest way to lose a member you never met.

Should my gym website offer a free trial or a day pass?

Yes, and it should be the main button on the site. Nobody signs a contract with a gym they have never walked into. A free week, a day pass or a first class free gives a nervous first-timer a low-commitment step, gets them through the door, and gives you a name and number to follow up. It converts far better than a contact form.

Do I have to show my membership fees on the website?

That is your call. Showing figures filters out people who were never going to join and cuts wasted calls. Hiding them gets more enquiries but a lower close rate. Whichever you choose, be straight about the contract itself: length, notice period, joining fee and whether it is month-to-month. Hidden terms are what kills trust in this industry.
