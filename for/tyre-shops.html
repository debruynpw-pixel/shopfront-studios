How people actually buy tyres

There are two customers and they behave completely differently.

The first is in trouble. Blowout on the way to work, a slow puncture that went flat in the parking lot, or a roadworthy that failed on tread depth and the car needs to be back at the testing station tomorrow. This person searches "tyres near me" or "tyre fitment Roodepoort" on a phone, sometimes standing next to the car. They are not comparing. They want the nearest place that is open and can do it now. If your hours are not on the page they will not gamble on the drive — they will phone the next listing.

The second is comparing on size. They already know they need 205/55 R16 or 265/65 R17 for the bakkie, because they walked out and read the sidewall. Now they are shopping that number across three or four shops, checking who has stock, in which brand, and what the fitted price is. They search the size itself — often literally typing "205/55 R16 price" — and whoever gives them a straight answer fastest usually gets the job.

Notice what neither of them cares about: your founding story, the mission statement, the hero video of a tyre rolling in slow motion. Tyre buying is a specification purchase. The brand storytelling that works for a restaurant does nothing here.

What does move them is stock and speed. "Continental, Bridgestone, Dunlop and Goodyear in stock, fitted while you wait, Saturdays till 13:00, walk-ins welcome" outperforms three paragraphs of polish. And there is a real shortcut most shops in South Africa already use on WhatsApp without putting it on their website: let people photograph the writing on the tyre and send it in. Half of drivers cannot read a tyre size and are embarrassed to admit it. Take that friction away and the enquiries climb.

Last thing — you sell more than tyres, and most sites hide it. Wheel alignment, balancing, batteries, exhausts, shocks and brakes are all things people search for separately. If the alignment work is invisible on your site, that search goes to the shop that mentions it.

How a fitment centre gets found in South Africa

Urgent local searches are decided in the map pack — the three business listings that appear above the normal results with a map next to them. That is your Google Business Profile, not your website, and for a tyre shop it is the highest-leverage thing you own. Correct category (tyre shop, plus secondary categories for wheel alignment and auto parts), accurate hours with holiday overrides, photos of the actual bays, and reviews coming in steadily. Our guide to setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa covers the whole process, and every branch needs its own separate profile — not one profile for the group.

That is where the branch pages earn their keep. Each branch page carries the exact name, address and phone number of its own Google profile, and links to it. Google matches the two, and each branch ranks in the suburb it actually sits in. One combined contact page tells Google nothing about where you are, so it ranks you nowhere in particular.

On the search terms: people type sizes and suburbs. "205/55 R16 Centurion", "tyre fitment near me", "wheel alignment Boksburg", "bakkie tyres Roodepoort", "tyres open Saturday". Your pages should carry those phrases in plain language — a page or section for alignment, one for batteries, one for bakkie and 4x4 sizes, one per branch suburb. This is not keyword stuffing, it is just describing what you sell using the words your customers use.

Reviews matter more here than in most trades because the purchase is fast and trust-light — a customer with a flat tyre will pick four stars nearby over an unknown listing every time. Ask at the till, while the wheel nuts are being torqued. And keep the site light: an urgent mobile search on a bad signal is your core traffic, and every extra second of load time is somebody phoning the next shop.

Frequently asked questions

What does a tyre shop website actually need?

The brands you stock, a way to enquire by tyre size, the services you do beyond tyres, your opening hours including Saturday, whether you take walk-ins, and a WhatsApp button. Most visitors have a flat tyre or a failed roadworthy right now, so the page must answer them in seconds on a phone.

Can customers search or enquire by tyre size?

Yes. We build a size enquiry field where someone types 205/55 R16 and sends it straight to your WhatsApp or inbox, along with an option to photograph the sidewall if they cannot read the numbers. You reply with availability and a price. No stock system needed unless you want one.

Should the website show my prices?

That is your call and it depends on how often your supplier pricing moves. Many fitment centres prefer an enquiry by size so they can quote on what is actually on the shelf that week. We can build it either way, or show indicative ranges on your fastest-moving sizes only.

We have three branches. Do we need three pages?

Yes. One generic contact page splits your local search visibility three ways. Each branch gets its own page with its own address, hours, phone number, photos and map, matched to its own Google Business Profile, so each one ranks for searches in its own suburb.
