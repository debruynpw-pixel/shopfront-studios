How people actually choose a dentist

Your traffic splits cleanly into two people, and most practice websites are built for neither.

The first is in pain. A cracked molar on a Saturday, an abscess that has been building all week, a filling that came out. They are searching "emergency dentist Randburg" or just "toothache" on a phone, one-handed, and their attention span is about ten seconds. They are not reading your practice philosophy. They want to know: are you open, will you see me today, and what is the number. Whoever answers that first gets the patient. Everyone else gets nothing, because the problem is solved the moment someone says yes.

The second has been deliberating for months, sometimes years. Implants, aligners, a crown they know they need, whitening before a wedding. This person reads everything. They compare three or four practices, they read Google reviews properly — not the star rating, the actual sentences, and especially the middling ones — and they are trying to work out two things: is this person good at this, and will I be treated decently while it happens. They will look for the dentist's qualifications and how long they have been practising. They will look at the team photos. They will notice if there aren't any.

Both check medical aid. Dental benefits are capped and confusing on most schemes, and patients know that the difference between a contracted and non-contracted practice can be significant out of pocket. If a Discovery or Bonitas member cannot tell where they stand, that is a reason to look elsewhere before they have even considered whether you are any good.

And underneath both journeys is the thing nobody puts on the website: a lot of people are genuinely afraid of the dentist. Not mildly reluctant — properly anxious, often because of something that happened to them as a child. Those patients have usually postponed care for years and feel embarrassed about the state of their teeth on top of being scared. The site that acknowledges this plainly, and says what the practice does about it, is the one they book.

Why most dental websites lose the patient

No emergency path, so a patient in pain scrolls, gives up and calls the next practice.

A dedicated emergency section visible the moment the page loads on a phone: tap-to-call number, whether you hold same-day slots, what to do right now for a knocked-out tooth or swelling, and what happens after hours or over a long weekend. This is the single highest-value block on a dental site.

Medical aids are not listed, or hidden behind "we submit to all major schemes".

Name them — Discovery, Bonitas, Momentum, Bestmed, GEMS, whichever apply — and be clear about whether you are contracted, whether you submit claims directly, and what a patient should expect when a benefit runs out mid-year. Vague answers on money make people assume the worst.

Treatment pages written in clinical language that raises anxiety instead of settling it.

"Endodontic therapy" tells an anxious patient nothing except that something serious is about to happen to them. Write it as a root canal, describe what the appointment is actually like, how long it takes, how the area is numbed and what the recovery involves. Accurate and calm is not the same as dumbed down.

No photographs of the team, so the practice reads as anonymous and interchangeable.

Real photographs of the dentists, hygienists and front desk staff, with names, plus the rooms themselves. Someone who is afraid is trying to work out whether these are people who will be kind to them. A stock photo of a smiling model with perfect teeth answers the opposite question.

What we build into a dental practice site

Emergency appointment path

A persistent, unmissable route for patients in pain: tap-to-call, same-day availability, first-aid guidance for common dental emergencies, and clearly stated after-hours and public holiday arrangements.

Medical aid schemes, named

The schemes you are contracted with, listed plainly, with a straight explanation of direct claim submission and what happens when annual dental benefits are exhausted. Easy for your reception staff to update.

Plain-language treatment pages

A page per treatment — fillings, root canals, crowns, extractions, implants, aligners, hygiene visits — describing what happens, how long it takes, what it feels like and what recovery involves. Written to inform, not to sell.

Anxiety and sedation information

An honest page for nervous patients: how the practice handles dental anxiety, what local anaesthetic and any sedation options actually involve, whether longer appointments can be arranged, and a way to flag anxiety when booking so the team knows before you arrive.

Dentist bios with credentials

Each practitioner's name, photograph, qualification and university, years in practice, special interests, and HPCSA registration number, alongside the practice number. Facts, presented factually.

Online booking and practical details

A mobile booking flow that captures treatment type, medical aid and preferred times, plus hours, directions, on-site or street parking, wheelchair access and whether the rooms are on ground level.

How a dental practice gets found in South Africa

Emergency dental searches are almost entirely local and almost entirely mobile, which means your Google Business Profile does more work than your homepage does. When someone in Brooklyn or Bellville searches "emergency dentist near me", the map results appear first with your hours, phone number and reviews attached — and a large share of those patients call straight from that listing without ever opening a website. Getting the profile complete, correctly categorised, with accurate hours over holidays and a steady flow of genuine reviews is the highest-leverage local SEO you can do; our guide to setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa walks through it, verification included.

The website carries the considered patient. That person searches by treatment and by suburb — "dental implants Pretoria East", "clear aligners Durban North" — so each treatment deserves its own page rather than one long services list. Those pages should load quickly on mobile data, mention the areas you serve in normal sentences, and answer the practical questions in the text: how many visits, how long between them, what the recovery is like.

The constraint to be honest about: the HPCSA's ethical rules restrict how registered practitioners may advertise and canvass for patients in South Africa, and that shapes what a dental site can legitimately claim. We build these as informational and access-focused — factual descriptions of treatments, real credentials, easy booking — rather than promotional. That means no superlatives, no comparisons with other practitioners, no guarantees about clinical outcomes, and careful handling of before-and-after imagery, which is appropriate for some treatments and not others and always requires proper patient consent. We are web designers, not your compliance advisers, so check your final wording and image use against the current HPCSA guidelines or with SADA. Frequently asked questions

What should a dental practice website include in South Africa?

It needs an obvious emergency path with a phone number and same-day availability, the medical aid schemes the practice is contracted with listed by name, plain-language explanations of common treatments, dentist bios with qualifications and HPCSA registration, online booking, information on anxiety and sedation options, and practical details like parking, access and after-hours arrangements.

How do you make a dental website less frightening for anxious patients?

Use ordinary words instead of clinical terms, describe what actually happens in an appointment step by step, say plainly how pain is managed and what sedation options exist, show real photographs of the team and the rooms, and make it easy to raise anxiety when booking. Most dental fear is fear of the unknown, so describing the unknown removes a large part of it.

Can a dentist show before-and-after photos on their website?

It depends on the treatment and on the professional rules that apply. The HPCSA's ethical rules restrict advertising and canvassing by registered practitioners, and images that function as promotion of clinical outcomes are treated more strictly than factual illustration of a procedure. Use before-and-after images only where it is appropriate for that treatment, always with proper patient consent, and confirm your approach against the current HPCSA guidelines.

Why does an emergency page matter for a dental practice?

A patient with a broken tooth or severe toothache is searching on a phone, in pain, and will call whoever answers first. If your site makes them scroll past service pages to find a number, they call the practice that put it at the top. An emergency path with a tap-to-call number, today's availability and after-hours arrangements captures those patients.
