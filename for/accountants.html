How a business owner actually chooses an accountant

There are two moments when someone goes looking for you, and they feel completely different.

The first is panic. It is late February or late August, provisional tax is due, and the person realises their records are a shoebox and a bank app. Or a letter from SARS has arrived about an outstanding return from 2023. Or the current bookkeeper has stopped answering. This person is not comparison shopping. They want someone who will answer today.

The second is admin. A new company has been registered at CIPC, and now there is a list: SARS income tax registration, VAT if they cross the threshold, PAYE and UIF once staff arrive, a beneficial ownership filing. This person is calmer but far more thorough, and they will read four or five firms properly before phoning one.

Both of them are asking the same four questions, and almost no accounting website in South Africa answers them.

Do you handle businesses my size? A sole proprietor turning over R40 000 a month does not want a firm that clearly serves listed subsidiaries, and a group with three entities and forty staff will not phone a site that reads like it was built for freelancers. Say who you serve. It filters out the wrong enquiries and pulls in the right ones.

Are you actually registered? Small business owners talk to each other, and everyone knows someone whose bookkeeper vanished with a year of source documents. SAICA, SAIPA, SAIBA, CIMA, and your SARS tax practitioner registration are trust signals that carry real weight here.

What happens after I say yes? The single biggest reason a warm enquiry goes cold is that nobody explains the next step. Write the onboarding out. First call, engagement letter, list of documents, SARS eFiling profile transfer, first monthly pack in your hands by a stated date.

Will you actually reply? This is the one everyone worries about and nobody says out loud. The industry has a reputation for silence in February. A stated response time and a WhatsApp number that a human answers does more for conversion than any redesign.

Why most accounting websites lose the enquiry

The homepage says "we offer a full range of accounting services to businesses of all sizes" and nothing more specific appears anywhere.

Rewritten around what you actually do all day. Monthly bookkeeping for owner-managed businesses in Bellville. Payroll for companies with five to fifty staff. VAT reconciliation and SARS dispute resolution. Concrete work described concretely, which is also what ranks.

Nothing indicates which client size you serve, so both the freelancer and the fifty-staff manufacturer assume you are not for them.

A plain section stating who you work best with, ideally with a line or two on typical clients: startups in their first two years, established retailers, professional practices, body corporates. Prospects self-select, and your enquiry quality goes up immediately.

No professional body logo, no registration number, no tax practitioner number. The site could belong to anyone with a spreadsheet.

Professional body membership, practice number and SARS tax practitioner registration displayed in the footer and on the about page. Partner profiles with qualifications and years in practice. It takes ten minutes to add and it changes who phones you.

The only way to start is a contact form that asks for name, email and message, then sends nothing back.

Multiple doors. Tap-to-call, WhatsApp, a short qualifying form that asks entity type and roughly how many transactions a month, and a booking link for a first consultation. Every submission triggers an automatic reply that tells them when to expect a human.

Not a word about fees, so the visitor assumes the worst and phones the firm that at least explained how it charges.

Fee structure explained without publishing a rate card if you would rather not. Say whether you work monthly, per return or hourly, what a standard monthly package covers, and what sits outside it. Answer the anxiety, then invite the call.

Onboarding is invisible, so a client who said yes on Tuesday is still not onboarded three weeks later.

A numbered onboarding page and a secure upload link you can send in one message. The client knows exactly which documents to send, sends them once, and your first month starts on time instead of a fortnight late.

What we build into an accounting firm site

A page per service

Monthly bookkeeping, annual financial statements, tax returns and provisional tax, payroll with PAYE and UIF, VAT registration and returns, CIPC company registration and annual returns, SARS dispute and objection handling. Each its own page, each able to rank on its own.

Professional body display

SAICA, SAIPA, SAIBA, ACCA or CIMA membership shown properly, with your practice number and SARS tax practitioner registration. Partner profiles with qualifications, years in practice and the industries they know best.

Secure document upload

An encrypted upload that puts bank statements, invoices and ID copies where they belong instead of in a WhatsApp thread. POPIA-aware handling, and one link you can send a new client instead of a five-message back-and-forth.

Fee structure clarity

A page that explains how you charge, what a monthly engagement includes, and what is billed separately. You decide whether to show numbers. Either way the prospect stops guessing, which is the actual conversion problem.

Client portal signposting

If you run Xero, Sage, Zoho, SimplePay or your own portal, the site gives existing clients an obvious login and new ones an explanation of what they will get access to. Fewer "where do I find my statement" emails during your busiest month.

Written onboarding steps

Step one, a thirty-minute call. Step two, engagement letter signed electronically. Step three, documents uploaded. Step four, eFiling profile transferred. Step five, first monthly pack. Written on the page, so the prospect can see the whole path before committing.

How an accounting practice gets found in South Africa

Accounting search here is local, seasonal and intent-heavy. People search accountant Centurion small business, tax practitioner near me, bookkeeper for small business Durban, SARS dispute help. Volumes spike hard in the weeks before the provisional tax deadlines and around the filing season opening, so being visible in January and July matters more than being visible in April.

Your Google Business Profile does most of the heavy lifting. For "accountant near me" searches the map pack sits above everything, and a profile with the right category, correct hours, a real office photo and a run of recent reviews will beat a better website attached to a neglected profile. Most practices we talk to have never claimed theirs or have not touched it since lockdown. Our guide to setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa walks through it, and we handle the setup as part of a build.

Service pages carry the rest. A single Services page cannot rank for both payroll services Randburg and CIPC annual return help. Separate pages can, and each one lands the visitor directly on the thing they searched for rather than on a homepage they must navigate.

Reviews are where accountants leave the most on the table. You have clients you have looked after for nine years who would write something in two minutes if asked. Ask in March, when the relief of a filed return is fresh. Reviews that mention the service by name, such as VAT or payroll, help the ranking for that exact search.

Then the plumbing: fast loading on mobile data, ProfessionalService and FAQ schema so AI answers and Google can quote your pages directly, and internal links between service pages so someone who arrived for tax discovers you also do payroll.

Frequently asked questions

Should we publish our fees on the website?

Publish the structure, not necessarily the numbers. Say whether you work on a monthly retainer, per return, or hourly, say what a monthly bookkeeping package typically includes, and say what falls outside it such as a SARS dispute or a CIPC annual return. That answers the question behind the question, which is whether the prospect can afford to phone you at all. Firms that explain their model get fewer time-wasting enquiries and more serious ones.

It depends on how many services you want separate pages for, whether you need secure document upload, and whether the site must signpost into an existing client portal like Xero, Sage or SimplePay. A solo bookkeeper serving twenty small businesses needs a different build to a five-partner practice handling audits and payroll. Do we need to display our SAICA, SAIPA or SAIBA registration?

Yes, and put it where people look rather than buried on an about page. Small business owners have been burnt by unregistered bookkeepers who disappeared with a year of records, so professional body membership and your SARS tax practitioner registration are among the first things a careful prospect checks. It is faster for onboarding, it keeps you defensible under POPIA, and it removes the most common reason a new client stalls two weeks after saying yes, which is that nobody told them exactly what to send or where to send it.
