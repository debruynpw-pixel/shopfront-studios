How pet owners actually arrive at a vet website

There are two people landing on your site and they behave nothing alike.

The first one is in trouble. A bull terrier has been hit by a bakkie outside the complex. A puppy got into snail bait. A boerboel's stomach has gone hard and tight and the owner has read enough to be terrified of the word bloat. It is 21:40 on a Sunday. That person is standing in a driveway with a torch in one hand and a phone in the other, and they are typing "emergency vet near me" while someone else holds the dog. They will not read your homepage. They will not scroll. They will scan for a number, and if they do not see one within about four seconds they will hit the back button and phone the next clinic on the list. Nothing else on your site matters to them.

The second one is calm and comparing. New puppy, new suburb, or unhappy with the last practice. They want to know if you do dentals and x-rays in-house or refer out, what your consultation hours are on a Saturday, whether you sterilise on a day-theatre basis, whether you board over December, and crucially what the vets are actually like. This person reads every Google review, twice. They look at the team page for a long time. Pet owners do not choose a clinic, they choose a person they are willing to hand their animal to, and that decision is emotional and made almost entirely on faces, tone and reviews.

A vet site has to serve both without compromise. Emergency contact that is unmissable for the first person, and depth, warmth and proof for the second.

Why most vet practice websites lose the call

The after-hours number is buried on the contact page, written inside an image, or missing altogether — so the emergency call goes to whichever clinic put theirs at the top.

A fixed emergency bar sits at the top of every page on the site, labelled after-hours, one tap to dial, and it stays there while the visitor scrolls.

No real service list, so an owner cannot tell whether you do dentals, digital x-rays, ultrasound, orthopaedic work or exotics, and they phone to ask — or they just don't.

Services split into three plain groups: routine, emergency and surgical, each item written the way owners say it, with a line on what happens in-house versus what gets referred.

No photos of the team. The site talks about compassionate care while the people giving it are invisible, which is the exact thing the nervous owner is trying to assess.

A team page with a proper photo, qualification, SAVC registration and special interest for every vet and senior nurse — the page that gets read longest on almost every clinic site we build.

No booking path, so every vaccination, check-up and script repeat comes through the phone and reception is stuck on hold-and-transfer all morning while real emergencies queue behind them.

Online booking for routine visits and a separate repeat request form for chronic scripts and prescription food, both landing in the inbox and on WhatsApp so the phone line stays open for the urgent stuff.

What we build into a veterinary practice site

Unmissable emergency contact

A sticky after-hours bar on every page with the on-call number, plus what to do on the way in. If you rotate on-call between vets or share an after-hours roster with a nearby practice, we build it so the number can be swapped in seconds.

Services split three ways

Routine — vaccinations, annual check, deworming, microchipping, sterilisation. Emergency — trauma, poisoning, bloat, snake bite, dystocia. Surgical — dentals, soft tissue, orthopaedic, imaging. Owners find their situation without guessing.

Team bios with photos

Each vet and nurse gets a face, a qualification, SAVC registration number and a line about what they love working on. This is what converts a nervous first-time owner into a booking.

Appointment booking

A form that captures owner, pet name, species and breed, reason for the visit and preferred day, so reception confirms rather than interrogates. Chronic patients and new clients get separate routes.

Repeat scripts and food orders

Chronic medication repeats and prescription diet orders — Hill's, Royal Canin, whatever you stock — requested online and collected at the counter, instead of three phone calls and a forgotten note.

Hours, boarding, directions

Opening hours listed per day including Saturday and public holidays, boarding and kennel availability with a December enquiry form if you offer it, and a map with the honest parking situation and which gate to use after hours.

How a vet practice gets found in South Africa

Almost every new client arrives through one of two searches: "vet near me" or "emergency vet [suburb]". Both of those are answered by Google's map results long before anyone reaches a website, which means your Google Business Profile does the first half of the job and the site does the second. Get the profile right — correct category, hours per day, after-hours note, photos of the practice and the team, and a steady flow of reviews. We wrote a full walkthrough of that in our guide to setting up a Google Business Profile in South Africa, and for a clinic it is the single highest-return hour of admin you will do this month.

Reviews carry more weight here than in most industries. Owners read them looking for one specific thing: what happened when it went badly. A calm, human reply from the practice on a hard review does more for a stranger's confidence than twenty five-star ratings. The site supports this by making it easy to leave one — a link after an appointment, not a QR code stuck on the counter that nobody scans.

On the search side, the pages that earn traffic are the specific ones. A page on sterilisation aftercare, one on what to do if your dog eats snail bait, one on tick fever season in your area. Owners google those phrases at 22:00 in a panic, land on your practice, and phone. Add suburb names where they are true — Fourways, Bellville, Hillcrest — because "vet in Bellville" is what people actually type, and load shedding schedules are worth a line too if your theatre or after-hours hours shift with the stage.

Frequently asked questions

Where should the after-hours emergency number go on a vet website?

In a fixed bar at the top of every page, tappable, with the words after-hours emergency next to it. Not on a contact page, not in the footer, not in an image. A person whose dog has just been hit by a car on Jan Smuts at nine at night will give your site about four seconds before they phone the next clinic on Google.

Can pet owners book appointments online?

Yes. Routine visits like vaccinations, annual check-ups, sterilisation consults and dentals can be booked through a form that captures the pet name, species, breed, reason for the visit and preferred day. It lands in your inbox and on WhatsApp so reception can confirm the slot instead of taking the same booking call fifteen times a morning.

Should the site list which vets work at the practice?

Yes, with photographs. Pet owners bond with a specific vet and often phone asking for that person by name. A short bio with each vet's qualification, SAVC registration and special interest, whether that is exotics, orthopaedics or feline medicine, does more for trust than any amount of copy about caring for your furry family.
